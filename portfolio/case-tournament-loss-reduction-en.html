Tournament Loss Structure Improvement

NSUSLAB Korea · Dec 2023 - Jan 2024

I kept the core goal of securing 200 offline finalists,
while redesigning the operating structure of a recurring event and cutting losses by roughly 50%.

Summary

An operations project that improved the loss structure of a recurring tournament without weakening the core target.

Problem
Certain buy-in ranges and formats from the prior year were generating outsized losses.
Goal
Keep the target of securing 200 offline finalists while reducing losses.
How
Reworked the schedule around lower-loss formats using prior-year metrics and participation patterns.
Outcome
Cut losses by roughly 50% versus the previous year's comparable event.

Background

One recurring event at NSUSLAB Korea had a clear operating objective: secure 200 players for an offline final event. But when I reviewed the previous year's results, I found that certain buy-in ranges and formats were creating disproportionate losses without delivering efficient participation. Repeating the same structure did not make sense.

Problem

The challenge was not simply to reduce losses. The core KPI of securing 200 offline finalists had to stay intact, and any adjustment still had to remain within internal operating guidelines. I needed a way to improve the economics of the event without sacrificing the central business objective.

  • Prior-year data showed outsized losses in specific buy-in ranges and formats.
  • The key KPI of securing 200 offline finalists could not be compromised.
  • Changes had to stay within internal operating guidelines.

Goals

  • Improve the loss structure of the recurring event.
  • Maintain the target of securing 200 offline finalists.
  • Create an operating plan that was realistic and executable within existing guidelines.

Approach

1. I re-read the prior year's metrics to find where losses were concentrated

I reviewed historical data across buy-in ranges, formats, and participation flow to identify where the event structure was breaking down. Instead of only checking final results, I compared which formats were contributing to the core target with less downside.

2. I reorganized the schedule around lower-loss formats

I kept the target KPI fixed, but restructured the event schedule around formats that had shown relatively lower losses in the previous year. The point was not to maximize one number in isolation, but to improve the economics without damaging the participation goal.

3. I adjusted promotional visibility within internal guidelines

Within the allowed operating framework, I gave the target event stronger visual prominence to encourage more efficient click-through and participation. This helped support the revised structure instead of relying on schedule changes alone.

Outcome

  • Maintained the core objective of securing 200 offline finalists.
  • Reduced losses by roughly 50% versus the previous year's comparable event.
  • Built a stronger habit of treating operational issues as solvable structure problems, not just reporting tasks.

What I Learned

This project taught me that numbers matter most when they are tied to decision structure. In operations, the real job is often not to chase a cleaner metric in isolation, but to protect the main objective while redesigning the parts of the system that are causing waste. That way of thinking later shaped how I approached planning work as well.
